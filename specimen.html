Qalam Badi

A proportional Bahá'í text family, derived from Courier Badi and leaning toward the hand of Mishkín-Qalam — while keeping the uniform monolinear stroke the Courier model produced.

يا بهاء الأبهى
Yá Bahá'u'l-Abhá — the invocation Mishkín-Qalam calligraphed
What changed

The cell is gone

Courier Badi is strictly monospace: every glyph, from the period to the m, occupies the same 1228-unit cell. Qalam Badi keeps all of its outlines and changes only how much room each one is given.

The seed line is the monospace master this project starts from. Nothing about the letters differs between the two lines except their widths and, for a handful of single-stem letters, the reach of their serifs.

The module

Everything is measured in nuqta

The seed master already draws the Arabic nuqta — the calligrapher's dot — at 271×289 units, and the Latin period at 270×310. One dot module, shared across the scripts, sitting there unused. Denominating the spacing in it is what lets Latin, Cyrillic, Greek and Arabic share a single rhythm instead of four unrelated ones.

Also inherited The monolinear pen measures 141 units — and it still does, everywhere in this font. The inherited alef stood 1252 units, 4.6 nuqta, which is naskh proportion; it now stands 3.6, between nastaʿlīq's 3 and where it started, and close to the 3.5 measured in Mishkín-Qalam's own Greatest Name.
Classification

Two distortions, opposite fixes

A monospace cell damages glyphs in two directions at once, and the two need different treatment.

Too narrow. The alef is a single 141-unit stroke parked in a 1228-unit cell. Nothing is wrong with the outline — re-fitting the advance is the whole fix.

Too wide. Courier's i is one 141-unit stem carrying serifs 910 units across. Its ink genuinely fills the cell, so re-spacing achieves nothing and i stays as wide as m. These need the outline narrowed — which is done by compressing the flanks either side of the stem, so the stem width, and therefore monolinearity, is mathematically untouched.

In the seed, every letter from J to w sat between 770 and 1112 units of ink — a range of 1.4× across the whole alphabet. That flatness is the typewriter. The ratio of i to m was 0.93; it is now 0.43, against roughly 0.35–0.40 for a conventional serif text face.

Arabic

Joins are pinned, not spaced

A joining edge is not a sidebearing. An initial, medial or final form carries its connector exactly on the advance edge, and giving that edge any sidebearing at all visibly pulls the script apart. Those edges are pinned to zero — and detected from the outlines themselves rather than a hand-kept table, so they stay correct when the Arabic is edited upstream. 643 glyphs carry a pinned join.

Arabic sits tighter than Latin here on purpose: in a nastaʿlīq hand the letters within a word nearly touch and the word gap carries the rhythm instead. That contrast survives the loss of stroke contrast, which is why it is doing the work in a monolinear face.
